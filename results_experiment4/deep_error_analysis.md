### Key Insights

1. **Run B is globally optimal but locally imperfect.** The 5 normalized PDW features
   (ToA, Freq, PW, AoA, Ampl) are sufficient for separating most emitters, but
   emitters with overlapping PDW distributions in this 5D space will always be
   challenging for any distance-based clustering algorithm.

2. **Over-segmentation dominates in high-density scenarios.** When many emitters are
   present (stare_high, scan_high: 15-30 emitters), HDBSCAN tends to split individual
   emitters into sub-clusters due to intra-emitter PRI variation creating local
   density variations within the true cluster.

3. **Under-segmentation occurs when emitters are physically indistinguishable.**
   Two different emitters with the same Frequency and Pulse Width settings will
   be merged by HDBSCAN because they occupy identical regions in the 5D PDW space.
   This is not a clustering failure — it reflects the limits of the feature space.

4. **Noise points correspond to ambiguous boundaries.** Pulses labeled as noise
   (-1) concentrate at cluster boundaries where emitter distributions overlap.
   Adding more dimensions (Run C) makes this worse; the 5D space represents
   the best trade-off between separability and the curse of dimensionality.

5. **The 5D PDW space has reached its information limit.** No feature engineering
   approach tested (PRI statistics, FFT, UMAP, additional derived features) could
   improve on the 5 normalized PDW baseline. Further gains would require
   either: (a) additional sensor-level features not present in the PDW data, or
   (b) sequence-aware models (e.g., transformers or RNNs) that can leverage
   the temporal ordering of pulses within a window.
